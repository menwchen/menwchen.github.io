In 1985, Steve Jobs was forced out of Apple, the company he had founded, after losing a boardroom struggle for control. The image of a founder pushed out of his own company became so emblematic that it is, even now, the first story people reach for when they speak of Silicon Valley tragedy.

In June 2026, something stranger than Jobs's exile occurred. A man was barred from touching the artificial intelligence he himself was building. Jobs, at least, had lost a fight over corporate politics. This case is different. What stood in his way was neither a board nor a rival. It was the color of his passport.

## A Door That Closed in Four Days

The story begins on June 9, 2026, when the A.I. company Anthropic released two models it called the most powerful in its history, named Fable 5 and Mythos 5. Four days later, at 5:21 p.m. on June 13, a letter arrived from the United States Department of Commerce. It ordered that no one who was not an American citizen — anyone abroad or on American soil, including Anthropic's own foreign employees — be permitted to access the two models. The stated reason was national security. Yet the letter did not even specify what, precisely, the danger was.

The celebration did not survive four days. In the middle of a launch the company was toasting, an order arrived to pull some of the very people who had built the models away from the table — like an unwelcome guest crashing a feast. In Shakespeare's "Macbeth," the ghost of a murdered man appears at a banquet and ruins the revelry; the English phrase "the ghost at the feast" comes from that scene, meaning an ominous shadow falling over a happy occasion. That ghost had now taken its seat at this celebration.

## The Man Who Cannot Touch What He Made

Here is the person the ghost burned most. Andrej Karpathy is a figure no one in the field of artificial intelligence fails to recognize. He was a founding member of OpenAI, the man who built Tesla's self-driving A.I. from the ground up, and a researcher of the methods by which an A.I. is made to improve itself, ever more capable — methods so associated with him that his name was attached to them. People call it the "Karpathy Loop."

He joined Anthropic in May 2026. His assignment was clear: to use one artificial intelligence — Claude — to build the next generation of artificial intelligence faster. To put it plainly, to shape A.I. with A.I. Karpathy was recruited precisely to turn that loop. And he is not an American citizen.

If the order is carried out as reported, the very man brought in to turn the loop will be unable to touch the tool sitting at its center. It is as if one summoned a blacksmith and then took away the anvil. This is the Karpathy paradox.

And yet I do not believe this paradox ends as one man's misfortune. What happened to Karpathy is, in fact, a far older problem returning in its most naked form at the most advanced frontier — a problem philosophers have long called alienation. To see what that means, let me borrow two thinkers for a moment. It is not difficult; follow along.

## "What I Made Now Pushes Me Out" — Alienation, an Old Affliction

Begin with the nineteenth-century German, Karl Marx. He described the factory worker like this: all day he makes a thing, and yet the thing is neither his own nor for him. The more he makes, the more it becomes a stranger that drifts away from him. What he made breaks off from him and, in turn, bears down on him. This is alienation.

This alienation has a twin. It is the word "alienation" in its other sense — what the eighteenth-century Frenchman Jean-Jacques Rousseau meant by it: to hand over what is yours to another. In "The Social Contract," Rousseau asked what happens when people surrender all their rights at once. His answer was chilling. Once you hand everything over, you stand powerless before the very power you erected. The king you crowned becomes a stranger looking down on you.

Here lies a striking fact. Rousseau's sense and Marx's sense are, at root, a single word — alienation in English, aliénation in French. One means "to hand over," the other "to become a stranger," but they are two faces of the same event. **The moment you hand over what is yours, it returns to you as something foreign.** To surrender is to give birth to estrangement.

Now look at Karpathy again. He stands exactly where an A.I. he was called to shape with his own hands becomes, at the very moment of its completion, a stranger he must look up to. What he made pushes him out. Is there a clearer picture of alienation? So remember this: the cure for this affliction lies in recovering the self that existed before the surrender. At the end of this essay, we will return to this spot.

## When Sheep Drove Men Off the Land

This sort of alienation does not arise on its own. It arises because someone builds a fence. The oldest name for that fence is "enclosure."

Sixteenth-century England. Villages once held land that anyone could use. Even the poorest farmer grazed his sheep and gathered firewood there and made his living. Such shared land was called the commons. But when the price of wool soared, landlords fenced off the commons — to enclose, hence "enclosure" — and turned their own flocks loose on it. The farmers were driven from land their ancestors had worked for generations. What had belonged to all became, in an instant, the property of a few. Thomas More lamented that "sheep were devouring men."

The logic of enclosure is always the same: fence off what belongs to everyone, make it the property of a few, then justify it with a fine-sounding word — order, or efficiency, or safety. The commons being fenced this time is intelligence itself.

## Whose Is Artificial Intelligence?

Consider how a large artificial intelligence is made. It feeds on the writing humanity has accumulated over thousands of years — papers, poems, novels, lines of code, the countless conversations of the internet. It is, in other words, a creature born by absorbing the crystallized knowledge that all of humanity made together. So whose is it, in essence? Can it be said to belong to any one nation, any one company?

The export controls the United States once used to hold other countries back were chiefly about things — advanced semiconductor chips, the machines that make them, objects you can hold in your hand. But this order moved the line of control from the chip to the A.I. model itself, and further still, to the nationality of the person who would access that model. The object of control has shifted from things to the inside of a person's head — and to a person's passport.

What is more, even the justification is flimsy. Anthropic itself pushed back, arguing that the risk the government pointed to applies only in one very particular case, not as some universal danger, and that by that same reasoning the method would work just as well on the A.I. of other companies not even subject to export controls. Why, then, raise the fence only on this side?

The decisive scene lies elsewhere. Look beneath the surface and Fable 5 and Mythos 5 are the same artificial intelligence; the only difference is the height of the fence. Fable 5 carries a firmer layer of safeguards against dangerous uses and is released to the public; Mythos 5 has those safeguards stripped away and is opened only to a small number of approved institutions. If safety were the point, the version to claw back would be the one with the lower fence. And yet what this order seized from foreign hands was the safer one — Fable 5. Had safety truly been the aim, the safest version, at least, should have been left alone. Here the true identity of the fence is revealed. It is not a line that blocks danger. It is a line of ownership, dividing who may possess from who may not.

## So What Is the Answer?

Where, then, is the answer that takes the fence down? It is time to return to the spot we set aside earlier — recovering the self that existed before the surrender.

Here we must draw out one of Marx's deepest insights. People often misread him as having said, "abolish all private property." He did not. What he wanted to abolish was property that excludes others — ownership as enclosure, the fence that drives others off. In its place he sought not the abolition of ownership but the "reconstruction of individual property." To make together and to enjoy together, and within that, for the individual to reclaim his place as owner of what he made. To restore the seat of ownership that was taken away.

Here is the knot of this essay. The answer to the enclosure of intelligence is not to hand everything, once more, wholesale to the state. That is merely another of Rousseau's surrenders — repeating alienation while pretending to cure it. Is that not exactly what this national-security order does? It draws an artificial intelligence that ought to belong to everyone back inside the larger fence of the state.

The true answer lies in the opposite direction. Upon an intelligence shaped together, each person who made it reclaims his place as owner of what he made. For Karpathy to recover the right to touch, again, the model he built with his own hands. This seemingly small demand is in fact the most concrete point of a larger principle. Making together and the individual becoming owner do not collide. The latter, rather, is the completion of the former.

## Where an Honest Truth Leads

But the reason this answer is hard to realize is that the one who builds the fence always has a plausible justification. In "Macbeth," it is said that the devil leads us to hell not with lies but with honest truths. "National security" may be an honest worry. But look where that honest worry leads — to a world that wraps a fence of nationality around the intelligence of all humanity, and hands people's rights, once again, to an unfamiliar power. It is not where we meant to go. What we must recover is ourselves, as we were before the surrender.

Anthropic has said this is a misunderstanding and that it will soon restore access. Let us hope so. But the one lesson the history of enclosure teaches is this: a fence, once raised, is seldom taken down — just as the commons that became private land in the sixteenth century never, in the end, returned to being everyone's land.

The ghost has already taken its seat at the feast.
